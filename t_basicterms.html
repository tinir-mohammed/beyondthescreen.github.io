techniques of cinema

basic terms

Auteur

The term auteur is the French word for “author”. In the context of cinema, it refers to a filmmaker who exerts a high level of stylistic control and autonomy over their projects, resulting in movies that are “stamped” with their personality. An auteur is known for their recognizable style or thematic preoccupations. A film auteur is akin to the author of a novel or the artist of a painting. An auteur is described in contrast to a “metteur en scene,” who translates a story in another medium to film.

Wes Anderson is a contemporary auteur, who uses symmetrical visuals, primary-colour palettes, offbeat characters and touches of absurdity to create movies that are distinctly his.

Diegesis

Diegesis refers to the world created in a film by the objects, events, spaces, and characters of the film. These not only include the events we see unfolding and the characters we see onscreen, but also the events that occur before and after the events of the film, characters that do not explicitly appear in the screen, as well as other things and attitudes that we infer from the film and use to construct its diegetic world.

In this scene from Psycho (1960) , the sound of the car being driven is a diegetic sound that happens in the world of the film, while the musical score accompanying the scene is a non-diegetic sound that only the audience hears.

Flashbacks and Flashforwards

Flashbacks and flashforwards are when the movie jumps back and forwards in diegetic time. When flashbacks or flashforwards are used, the order of the events presented in the film no longer match the actual order of events in its diegetic world.

Citizen Kane (1941) is perhaps the best known film that almost entirely uses flashbacks to tell its story. It follows a reporter as he tries to piece together the titular character’s life by interviewing several people from his life.The stills below show the character of Jedediah Leland, played by Joseph Cotten, who appears in the film both as an old Jedediah recounting past events as well as a young Jedediah working with Kane.

Genres

Genres refer to categorisation of films based on shared stylistic elements, narratives, characters, and conventions. Genres have been made necessary by the large numbers of films produced by the film industry. Main genres like drama, comedy, and action can be further divided into subgenres like courtroom drama, romance, and spoofs.

Mise-en-Scène

Mise-en-Scène, French for “placing on stage,” refers to all that is placed in front of the camera and that forms part of a scene’s setting: from the lighting, composition, décor, and props, to the actors, their make-up, their costumes, and their performance. These elements are manipulated by filmmakers to convey the space and time as well as a mood and vibe.

Story and Plot

Often used interchangeably, plot refers to the events of the film that occur in front of the camera and are seen by the audience, while story refers to everything about the film’s diegesis that an audience infers from viewing the plot of the film. The story of a film is hence more extensive than the plot of the film. The order, duration, setting of the events presented in the film are all part of the plot that a filmmaker can manipulate in order to convey the story.

Christopher Nolan engages the viewers of his film Memento (2000) by making them piece together the story from a plot that is delivered in a reverse chronological order.

Scene and Sequence

A scene is a segment of a film that conveys a few lines of action taking place in a single time and place, and with the same characters. A scene may also occur in different spaces or time, related by crosscutting. A sequence can be used to mean the same as a scene, but a sequence can also be longer and spread out over different spaces and times, like a montage sequence.

Shot

A shot, the basic unit of cinema, refers to a single continuous stream of images uninterrupted by editing. A film is made up of hundreds of shots that are stitched together to create scenes and sequences, which in turn create the film.
