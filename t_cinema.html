techniques of cinema

cinematography

Cinematography is the art of capturing the photographic images using a number of elements: quality, scale, framing and movement. These elements can be manipulated to influence how the audience perceives the scene being shot. For instance, the grainy black and white quality of The Battle of Algiers (1966) lends an air of authenticity.

Quality

Colour

Filmmakers in the earlier years were limited to only black-and-white, but advancements in technology allowed the use of colour in film. Colour can not only make a film more realistic and add glamour, but can be skilfully used to create aesthetic patterns and to establish character or emotion.

In Wings of Desire (1987), the cinematographer Henri Alekan has used black-and-white imagery to denote the angels’ point of view of the world, and colour imagery to show the human point view in which angels are not visible. Although the immortal angels see and hear everything, they are unable to partake in the joy and sadness of transient human life.

Contrast

Contrast is the ratio of the lightness to the darkness in an image. Low contrast, that is when the difference between the light and dark areas is little, is used to achieve natural lighting. High contrast, on the other hand, is usually used to create a dramatic effect in crime films and can be achieved with low-key lighting. A common cliche is using light and dark to represent good and evil respectively.

In this still from Schindler’s List (1993), the protagonist is illuminated as he speaks to his factory workers, highlighting him as their saviour.

Deep Focus and Shallow Focus

Deep focus is used to give equal importance to objects, actors, and spaces both near to and far from the camera. As seen in another still from Citizen Kane (1941), deep focus is used to draw the audience’s attention towards young Charles Foster Kane as he plays outside the window while his parents discuss his fate indoors.

The opposite of deep focus is shallow focus, which only brings into sharp focus one plane of the image, guiding the audience’s attention to some particular element in the plane. This still from The Godfather (1972) shows how shallow focus is used to suggest psychological introspection, as Al Pacino’s character becomes increasingly oblivious to his surroundings and braces himself for what he is about to do next.

Racking Focus

Racking focus is the technique where the focus of the lens is changes such that an element in the scene goes out of focus and another comes into focus. This is used to direct an audience through the scene and also links two or more objects in the scene. This is observed in the following clip from Who Framed Roger Rabbit (1988), where racking focus is used to show the newsreel inspiring Bob Hoskin’s character’s Eureka moment.

Rate

A film is normally shot at 24 frames per second. However, if the number of frames per second, or the frame rate, is increased, then we can effect what we call slow motion.

In this clip from Reservoir Dogs (1992), the main characters are shown in slow motion as we are introduced to them to make them appear cool and hip.

The opposite is also possible, i.e. decreasing the frame rate would effect fast paced motion. Frame rate can also be manipulated to achieve stop-motion, commonly seen in stop motion animation films like Coraline (2009) and Fantastic Mr. Fox (2009).

Zoom Shoot

A zoom shot is achieved by changing the focal length of the lens that is used to take the shot, as the shot is being taken, so that it looks like we are getting closer to or farther away from the subject.

The camera slowly zooms in on Jack Torrance (Jack Nicholson) to emphasise his unhinged mental state and heighten tension, in The Shining (1980).

Framing

Angle of Framing

A shot taken from above or below the subject of the shot is said to be taken at an angle. An angled shot helps establish a relation between the subject and the camera’s point of view, or even a relation between two subjects.

When Wesley (Cary Elwes) is arrested by Count Rugen (Christopher Guest) in The Princess Bride (1987), the high angle shot of him makes him appear all the more defiant in his vulnerable position as a prisoner under the Count, who is shot from a low angle that emphasises his authority.

Level of Framing

The level of framing is the height at which the camera takes a shot. Placing the camera near the ground achieves low-level framing, while placing it higher than the typical height produces high-level framing. Different levels of framing may be used to achieve pleasurable compositions or to draw the audience’s sympathy for characters, especially those at lower levels.

Low-level framing is used in this still from The White Balloon (1995) to evoke sympathy for the little girl who feels helpless in the midst of a crowd of men after having lost her money to a couple of snake charmers.

Canted Framing

In canted framing, also known as the dutch angle, the camera is not placed level; rather, one side is lower than the other. This causes the image to appear slanted. Canted framing is often used to signify danger and chaos.

We see the dutch angle being used to frame Sgt. Donowitz (Eli Roth) and the German Sgt. Rachtman (Richard Sammel) in Inglourious Basterds (2009), as the former prepares to bash the latter’s head with a club.

Following Shot and Re Framing

A following shot follows the subjects of the frame and keeps them onscreen by shifting the frame. The objective of such a shot is to keep the subjects of the frame at the centre of the audience’s attention. The camera may also slightly tilt or pan, that is reframe, to keep the subjects centered. An example is seen in this clip from Get Out (2017) where the couple is followed by the frame.

Point-of-View Shot

A point-of-view shot is taken by placing the camera approximately where the character’s eyes would be, giving us a view of what they are seeing in an unobtrusive manner. Point-of view shots may be preceded or succeeded by shots of the character to establish whose point of view we are looking at. This is one way of allowing the audience to identify with the characters on screen.

A good example of this is the following scene from the gangster film The Untouchables (1987), where we see a point-of-view shot of a knife-wielding thug breaking into Malone’s (Sean Connery) house.

Wide-Angle Lens

Wide-angle lenses are short focal length lenses that emphasise the amount of space in a shot by distorting straight lines near the edge of the frame and exaggerating the distance between the foreground and background. This makes it convenient to use wide-angle lenses for shots of closed spaces like rooms with limited space.

However, since wide-angle shots have distorted lines near the edges of the frame, it look less naturalistic in shots of close spaces and often find use in unrestrained open spaces with no lines near the edges. This shot from Amélie (2001) demonstrates the use of a wide-angle lens in an open space.

Scale

The scale of the object in the frame can influence how we perceive the object. The scale can either get us up close and intimate with the object, or make the object desolate and consumed by its surroundings.

Extreme Long Shot

An extreme long shot is one in which the scale of the object is tiny. Such shots can be of buildings, a landscape, a crowd of people, or any such scene. Extreme long shots are often used as establishing shots.

This still from The Lobster (2015) show us the picturesque setting in which the protagonist and his two acquaintances are sitting together and talking.

Long Shot

In a long shot, the scale of the objects shown is small. When there are people in the frame, they are usually shown head to toe within the frame, as seen in this image from The White Balloon (1995). This is a convenient shot that shows full-body action without the need to reframe, and hence finds common use in genres like musicals and action films.

Medium Long Shot

A medium long shot has objects of four to five feet fill the screen entirely. Human subjects are shown from their head to their knees. Such shots are also called plain plan américain, because they found heavy use in westerns. Medium long shots were the closest you could get to an actor with their heads and holstered weapons within the frame.

The following medium long shot is from Woody Allen’s Annie Hall (1977).

Medium Close-Up

In a medium close-up, the scale of the object is large. Human subjects shown from the chest up will fill up the screen, as seen here in a still from the Norwegian film, Headhunters (2011).

Close-Up

A close-up shows an object in quite a large scale. A person’s head, or some other object of similar size, would fill up the screen. A close-up does not necessarily have to include people, and can be of other objects as well.

This still from the first sequence of A Clockwork Orange (1971) is a close-up of Alex (Malcolm McDowell) with an evil smirk on his face. This shot tells us from the get-go what a terrible person Alex is.

Movement

The motion of a camera can be fluid and smooth or it can be rapid and disorienting. Camera motions are used to allow the audience to take in a scene, follow the characters on screen, or even act as a way of effecting a transition between scenes.

Crane Shot

A crane shot is one that is taken by placing the camera on some crane-like device above the ground and sweeping it through the air in some direction. Crane shots are often those shots that cannot be easily achieved by a person. Crane shots may be used as establishing shots.

The crane shot is used in a spectacular fashion in this clip from Django Unchained (2012) to reveal to the audience the extent of the bloodbath and chaos of a massacre.

SteadiCam Shot

A Steadicam shot, or a handheld shot, is taken with a camera supported by the camera operator’s body who either holds it with their hands or uses a gyroscopic stabiliser and a harness. Although handheld cameras produce jerky and unstable images, the relative freedom afforded by them allow their use for motion tracking shots. Modern gyroscopic stabilisers minimise isolate the camera from the imperfections in the camera operator’s movements and allow smoother images.

A handheld shot follows Mr. Blonde (Michael Madsen) in this scene from Reservoir Dogs (1992), as he steps out of the warehouse, heightening fear and trepidation about what he might do next to the policeman who is held in the warehouse.

Pan

A camera is said to pan when it moves left or right and scans the space horizontally. A pan can be used to follow motion, reveal off-screen space, or even connect two or more objects in the scene. The speed of a pan may also be exploited for dramatic purpose. A pan may be accompanied by other motions like a zoom or movement of the camera base.

In this scene from North By Northwest (1959), the camera pans away from Cary Grant’s character in the UN building to reveal a man who has tailed him into the building.

tilt

A tilt is like a camera pan, except the camera moves up or down, scanning space vertically. A tilt can be used to heighten tension and suspense as the audience does not know what the camera may uncover, or when the camera will stop. Tilts can be used, for example, to introduce characters, where the frame moves from the feet to the face to reveal the character’s identity in a suspenseful manner.

A tilt here is used to mimic Andy Dufresne’s (Tim Robbins) point of view as he looks at the façade of the prison building and is taken into it, in The Shawshank Redemption (1994).

Tracking Shot

A tracking shot achieves a mobile framing that moves in some direction, following some action through space. There are a number of ways to achieve tracking shots: mounting the camera on a dolly, using tracks, or using a moving vehicle, that smoothly follows the action being filmed. Advances in steadicam technology also make it possible for handheld cameras to record spontaneous action.

A pioneering use of the Steadicam for tracking action was in Stanley Kubrick’s The Shining (1980). The Steadicam in this case was placed on a mount modeled on a wheelchair to take this famous tracking shot of Danny pedalling throughout the Overlook Hotel:

Whip Pan

A whip pan is a rapid motion of a camera to one side, so rapid that the images become blurry streaks of lines. Whip pans are used to effect transitions between scenes and may also be used to denote rapidly unfolding events. Whip pans stand out due to their rapid and flashy nature, and find common use in action or comedy films, as seen in this clip from Shaun of The Dead (2004).
