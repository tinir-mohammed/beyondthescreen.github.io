techniques of cinema

mise-en-scène

Mise-en-scène is the design aspect of a film’s production which can be used to convey the space and time of the film’s diegesis, the mood and theme, and also the character’s state of mind.

Décor

Décor refers to the objects within the setting of a scene. Décor can be used to convey the dominant theme and mood of a scene, and amplify characters’ emotions.

In the movie Fanny and Alexander, the rich red and green interiors of Alexander’s grandmother’s house convey a feeling of warmth and homeliness, a space where Alexander feels safe and loved. On the other hand, the cold white interiors of his stepfather’s house is synonymous with the cold and distant way he feels towards his stepfather.

In earlier years, rear projection was used to set films in exotic locations and sets which would have been difficult to recreate for economical, logistical, and safety reasons. In rear projection, action occuring in the foreground is combined with a background shot elsewhere, on location. This technique has been used to render scenes with stunts and more commonly, for scenes taking place in vehicles.

North By Northwest (1959) uses rear projection in the scenes taking place in cars and trains, as well as for when the protagonist is attacked by a crop duster.

Lighting

Adjusting the direction, intensity, and quality of lighting can draw attention to certain parts of the scene’s composition, bring out colours in different ways, and can be be used to create the scene’s mood. Films like documentaries may use natural lighting for a touch of authenticity. However, most narrative films use what is known as the three-point lighting and variations of it.

Three-point lighting

Three-point lighting consists of a backlight, which brings out the actor (or other object) from their background, a key light that highlights the actor, and a fill light from the opposite side that softens the key light’s shadow.

High-Key Lighting

Raising the fill light to the same level as the key light creates high-key lighting, which makes scenes brighter and with minimum shadows, as seen in the following still from the surrealist comedy The Discreet Charm of The Bourgeoisie (1972). High-key lighting sees greater use in comedies and musicals.

Low-Key Lighting

Low-key lighting uses minimal fill light, resulting in high contrasts and dark shadows. This is used to create suspenseful and dramatic scenes seen in crime and drama. Low-key lighting is used extensively in The Godfather (1972), as seen below.

Space

Deep and Shallow Space

When important elements in the frame are placed near and far from the camera, the elements are said to be staged in deep space. The distance between the elements create a sense of depth. However, the elements do not all need to be in focus, which is necessary for what is known as deep focus. The opposite is staging in shallow space, in which key elements are placed so close to each other that there is little depth.

In Psycho (1960), the position of Norman Bate’s house from the motel emphasises the distance that he is running.

Frontality

Frontality refers to when key elements, most often actors, are placed face-to-face with the camera. This technique is not used frequently since it breaks the audience’s illusion that they are looking in on a separate world and implies that the characters are aware of being watched. Frontality is sometimes accompanied by direct address, in which a character looks at and addresses the audience. Direct address is a form of what is commonly known as “breaking the fourth wall”.

Woodie Allen’s Annie Hall (1977) has many instances of frontality and direct addresses in which his character lets the audience in on his thoughts and takes them through the story.

Matte Shot

Before the advent of special effects and computer generated imagery, the matte shot was a popular technique whereby two images were combined in a single image using an optical printer. Matte shots were used to add elements to realistic images, or to make fantastical scenes.

Matte shots are found aplenty in Alfred Hitchcock’s films, like North by Northwest (1959). This shot of the interior of the UN Building is a combination of a matte painting and real-life images of people walking around.

Offscreen spaces

Offscreen spaces, as the term implies, are spaces that are out of the frame but are part of the film’s diegesis. Offscreen spaces are brought to the viewer’s attention when the characters on screen react to some other character or event happening offscreen, or by some offscreen sound. This can be used to create suspense in horror, crime, and drama films.

Costume

One of the most visible part of a mise-en-scène is costume. It is simply the clothes and makeup worn by the characters in a film. Costume can be used to signify the characters, the differences between characters, and help establish the era in which the film is set.

In the film The Princess Bride (1987), the costumes of the characters play an integral role in creating the fantasy adventure diegesis of the film.

Acting

Acting can be described as the most essential component of a film. To successfully translate the script to action, an actor needs a variety of skills: emotional range, physical expressivity, vocal projection, the ability to employ dialects and accents, and so on. Sometimes actors may also need training in specific skills for certain roles, like martial arts for an action film or singing for a musical.
