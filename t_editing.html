techniques of cinema

editing

Editing is the process of combining disparate shots together to create relationships between them. Editing is essential to stitch shots together to construct the narrative time and space. Some forms of editing are discreet, while others more obvious.

Devices

Transitions
cheat cuts

A cheat cut is a type of continuity editting that purports to show continuous time and space from one shot to the next, but actually mismatches the positions of figures or objects in the scene. Cheat cuts were often employed to disguise the difference in the heights of shorter leading men and tall leading ladies.

In the following series of stills from Shadow Of A Doubt (1943), the extreme close-up of female protagonist’s hands holding the ring reveals the crucial writing inside the ring. But it does not match up with how she is holding the ring in the shots before and after this shot.

crosscutting

Crosscutting, also called parallel editing, alternates between shots from two or more lines of action occurring in different diegetic spaces, simultaneously. In most but not all cases, the lines of action are occuring at the same time. Crosscutting is used to connect the two events that are occurring and the characters therein.

In this clip from Snatch (2000), crosscutting is used to draw parallels between the coursing and Tyrone (Ade) being chased by two thugs, likening him to the hare and his pursuers to the greyhounds.

cut-in and cut-away

A cut-in happens when we instantaneously shift from a distant view to a closer one, and vice-versa. In this scene from A Man Called Ove (2015), a distant shot shows us the erratic driving of the car that others on the street see, while a cut-in reveals Parvaneh (Bahar Pars) in the car struggling to drive correctly under Ove’s (Rolf Lassgård) supervision.

dissolve

A dissolve is a transition from one shot to another brought about by fading out the first shot and fading in the next, such that for a moment the two shots are superimposed on each other. Dissolves can be used simply as a transitional element, or in more creative ways, such as to denote hallucinatory states or the passage of time.

Dissolves are used in this sequence from The Shawshank Redemption (1994) as the character looks for a tree and a package buried underneath it.

Iris

An iris is a round, moving mask that closes in to end a scene or focus on a detail (iris-in), or open out to reveal a scene or more space around a detail (iris-out).

At the beginning of Shock Corridor (1963), an iris-out is used to reveal to the audience the eponymous corridor, with the protagonist introducing himself in the voice over.

Establishing Shot/Re-establishing Shot

Establishing shots reveal to the audience the setting of a scene, the characters therein, and the relationships between them These are typically the first few shots of a scene and are taken in extreme long shots or long shots.

The audience is introduced to the dry and hot Texas environs in some beautiful long shots at the very beginning of No Country For Old Men (2007), hence establishing the diegetic place of the film.

Shot/Reverse Shot

In this form of editing, multiple scenes are edited together such that they show characters alternately, typically to show them and their reactions in a conversation situation. Shots such as over-the-shoulder shots, angled shots, and left/right alternating shots can be used to achieve shot/reverse shots.

One example among many is illustrated by the following stills from Léon: The Professional (1994) which show Léon (Jean Reno) conversing with Mathilda (Natalie Portman).

Superimposition

Superimpositions are achieved by exposing multiple images on the same film strip. Superimpositions are not used for transitions (as in the dissolve) but for other creative purposes: to show the same actor playing two characters in the same frame, to express subjective or intoxicated vision, or to introduce other diegetic elements to the screen.

In this (yet another!) example from Citizen Kane (1941), newspaper headlines are superimposed on images of Kane’s wife performing to demonstrate the burgeoning ‘success’ of her singing career.

Matches

Match editing, a major element of continuity editing, is used to draw connections between elements in subsequent shots. The connections are implied by the situation portrayed in the scene. When done well, match editing can produce a seamless cinematic experience.

Eyeline Match

In eyeline matches, the shots are edited such that the first shot shows a character looking at something, and the second cuts to what the character is looking at.

Cinema Paradiso (1988) contains many shots of cinema-goers eyeline matched with shots of the screen, to show what they are attentively watching.

Graphic Match

Graphic matching uses a similarity in some compositional element (like shape or colour) to achieve a smooth transition between two shots. Such matching can connect two otherwise disconnected scenes, or establish a relationship between two scenes.

This clip from Psycho (1960), showing an example of a graphic match, takes place right after Marion (Janet Leigh) has been stabbed in the shower. As the blood is washed away with the water, the camera focuses on the circular bathtub drain, which is then matched with her lifeless eye in the next shot.

Match on Action

A match on action edit cuts alternate views of the same action together at the same moment in order to make it seem uninterrupted. This allows the same action to be seen from multiple angles without breaking its continuous nature.

In this clip from Kill Bill: Vol. 1 (2003), match on action editing provides a dynamic view of the fierce sword-fight between the Bride (Uma Thurman) and her adversary.

Duration
Long Take

A long take, like the name suggests, is a shot that lasts longer than usual. Modern films have very small shot lengths, so any shot that lasts for more than a minute is generally considered a long take. These are usually taken with a moving camera and require choreography and rehearsal to get right.

The famous Copacabana shot in Goodfellas (1990) is a long shot that follows Henry (Ray Liotta) and his wife as he bypasses the regular entrance and leads her on an alternate path through the back kitchens in an assured, confident way.

Rhythm

Rhythm refers to the perceived rate and regularity of sounds, series of shots, and movements within the shots. The rhythm of a film is influenced by beat (or pulse), accent (or stress), tempo (or pace) etc. It is a combination of mise-en-scène, cinematography, sound, and editing, all of which contribute to the overall mood and effect of the film.

The prelude to the final showdown of The Good, the Bad and the Ugly (1966) runs for several minutes, as three men face each other in a triangle, waiting to see who will take the first step. One of the film’s theme songs is played in its entirety, from a slow, elegiac beginning to a frenzy crescendo that is abruptly cut off by the first gunshot. The slow mounting crescendo is paralleled by an increase in the editing rate.

Styles

The patterned use of transitions, matches, and durations can give rise to identifiable editing styles which can be associated with technological developments, historical moments, and national schools.

Continuity Style

Continuity editing seeks to maintain clear and consistent narrative structure by matching screen direction, position, and temporal relations from shot to shot. It maintains the viewer’s assumption that space and time are contiguous between successive shots. This is done by adhering to the “180॰ rule”, which is illustrated below. According to this rule, the camera must always remain on one side of the “axis of action”, i.e. an imaginary line drawn connecting the two major elements, usually the actors.

The “180॰ rule” ensures that characters on screen occupy consistent positions on the screen, aiding the audience in understanding the layout of the scene. This is observed in the following scene from The Godfather: Part II (1974).

Elliptical Style

In the elliptical editing style, subsequent shots omit parts of time, causing ellipses in the plot and diegetic time. One form of editing special to elliptical editing is the jump cut. The jump cut emphasises the constructed nature of film, which is opposite to the continuity style.

In this clip from Scott Pilgrim vs. the World (2010), jump cuts show Scott (Michael Cera) impatiently signing for his package just to go out with the female protagonist.
