main genres of cinema

drama


1. Biography

Biographies are films that dramatizes the life of a non-fictional or historically-based person or people. Such films show the life of a historical person and the central character's real name is used. Not to be confused with biopic, which is a motion picture based on the life (or lives) of a real, rather than fictional, person (or people) while biography is a person's life story, especially one published.

Examples of biographical films include Theory of Everything (2014) and Malcolm X (1992).

2. Crime drama, legal drama and Courtroom Movies

Character development based on themes involving criminals, law enforcement and the legal system. Courtroom films are more popular in TV, they’re generally films which cover the legal proceedings surrounding a crime. They can often incorporate elements from other crime sub genres, including ‘whodunit’ mysteries.

Examples include Sherlock Holmes (2009) and Nightcrawler (2014)

3. Farce

Generally, a farce is a nonsensical genre of drama, which often overacts or engages slapstick humor. The only aim of a farce film is to make the audience laugh through situations that are highly exaggerated, extravagant, and thus improbable.

Examples include The Pink Panther Strikes Again (1976) and Brain Donors (1992).

4. Historical drama

Historical drama (also known as a historical drama, period drama, costume drama, or period piece) films often focus focus on dramatic events in history. Usually close to or entirely factual, but sometimes not.

Examples include 12 Years A Slave (2013) and Dunkirk (2017).

5. Docudrama

Docudramas features dramatized re-enactments of actual events. In its core, it’s essentially historical facts while allowing a greater or lesser degree of dramatic license in peripheral details, and where there are gaps in the historical record. Dialogues are also incorporated in the actual words of real-life persons, as recorded in historical documents. Docudrama are often filmed the actual locations in which the historical events occurred.

Examples include Schindler’s List (1993) and Hidden Figures (2016).

6. Melodrama

This subtype of drama films that uses plots that appeal to the heightened emotions of the audience. Melodramatic plots often deal with "crises of human emotion, failed romance or friendship, strained familial situations, tragedy, illness, neuroses, or emotional and physical hardship". Film critics sometimes use the term "pejoratively to connote an unrealistic, pathos-filled, camp tale of romance or domestic situations with stereotypical characters (often including a central female character) that would directly appeal to feminine audiences".

Examples include Pride and Prejudice (2005) and 9½ Weeks (1986)
